# Brine & Fire Noodles

Source: Andrew Gruel, Substack – "Brine & Fire Noodles"

My guilty pleasure? A quick hit of pasta on a weeknight. I don’t overdo carbs, but when I want noodles, I give in. I am not talking heavy cream sauces or goopy mac n cheese — but something sharp, briny, with a punch to the mouth. Nothing better than a tomato sauce loaded with olives and umami, draped over just enough linguine to satisfy. So basically, that’s puttanesca.

Legend says this sauce came out of Naples — it’s fast, pantry-driven, aromatic enough to pull you in off the street. Whether that’s folklore or fact, it is still one of the most efficient flavor bombs in the Italian canon. And the key is anchovy. Melted gently into olive oil, it dissolves into savory goodness, no fishiness.

The biggest failure in weeknight pasta? Separation. Watery sauce at the bottom with wet noodles on top - no cohesion. The fix is a basic (and temporary) emulsification. Cook the pasta in slightly less water so it’s starchier, then transfer it directly into the sauce without rinsing (using tongs from pot to pot). Finish with butter and Parmigiano, add a splash of pasta water, and toss aggressively over heat until glossy. That’s how you get “cling”.

## Ingredients

- 1/4 cup extra virgin olive oil
- 1 lb organic linguine
- 4 to 6 oil-packed anchovy fillets
- 8 to 10 cloves of garlic, minced
- 2 teaspoons ancho chili powder or red pepper flakes
- 1/2 cup pitted black olives, chopped
- 1/2 cup chopped Kalamata olives
- Optional handful of green olives
- 1/4 cup capers, rinsed and chopped
- 1 large 28 oz can whole peeled tomatoes, crushed by hand
- Pinch dried oregano
- 2 tablespoons sherry vinegar
- 4 tablespoons unsalted butter
- 1/2 cup freshly grated Parmigiano Reggiano
- 1/2 cup chopped flat-leaf parsley
- Fresh cracked black pepper

## Instructions

1. Bring salted water to a boil using slightly less water than usual.
2. In a wide sauté pan or Dutch oven, pour the olive oil. Add the anchovies and garlic, then turn the heat to medium.
3. Cook gently until the anchovies begin to melt. Stir in chili flakes and oregano and lightly fry for about 30 seconds.
4. Add the olives and capers. Sauté 2 to 3 minutes to let them bloom in the oil.
5. Add the tomatoes (crushed with your hands or a wooden spoon). Simmer 8 to 10 minutes until thickened.
6. Cook the linguine just shy of al dente.
7. Transfer the pasta directly into the sauce using tongs. Add a splash of pasta water and toss over medium heat. Cook a bit more if needed so the noodles and sauce come together.
8. Add the butter and toss until everything looks glossy and emulsified.
9. Remove from heat and stir in the Parmigiano, parsley, sherry vinegar, and plenty of fresh cracked black pepper.

## Variations & Add-Ins

Use this puttanesca-style base as a canvas. Optional upgrades:

- Seared shrimp finished in the sauce
- Crispy pancetta or guanciale
- Grilled swordfish chunks folded in at the end
- Oil-packed tuna gently flaked through
- White beans for a rustic version
- Soft-poached egg on top for richness
- Spoon of tomato paste caramelized before the tomatoes go in
- Splash of dry white wine reduced before adding tomatoes
- Pinch crushed fennel seed
- Calabrian chili paste for deeper heat
- Extra anchovy paste for more umami
- Lemon zest at the finish
- Torn fresh basil
- Arugula folded in off heat
- Fresh oregano instead of dried
- Toasted sourdough breadcrumbs in olive oil
- Crushed Marcona almonds
- Pine nuts toasted in butter
- Crispy garlic chips
- Bottarga shaved over the top
- Spoon of mascarpone for a silkier version
- Splash of good balsamic for subtle sweetness
- Charred cherry tomatoes folded in at the end
