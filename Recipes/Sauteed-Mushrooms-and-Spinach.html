Side

Sautéed Mushrooms and Spinach

Earthy mushrooms and wilted spinach in a garlicky butter pan sauce, a fast, versatile side that comes together in under 20 minutes.

Serves 4
Under 20 minutes
Stovetop

Ingredients

  • 16 oz cremini or mixed mushrooms, sliced
  • 5 1/4 oz fresh baby spinach
  • 2 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • 4 garlic cloves, minced
  • 3 tbsp dry sherry or white wine
  • 1/4 tsp red pepper flakes
  • 3/4 tsp kosher salt
  • 1/4 tsp black pepper
  • 1 lemon, juice only
  • 2 tbsp fresh parmesan, grated (optional)

Method

  1. Pat the sliced mushrooms dry with paper towels. Moisture kills browning, so don’t skip this.
  2. Heat a large skillet over medium-high. Add the olive oil and butter and let the foam subside.
  3. Add mushrooms in a single layer. Don’t stir for 3 to 4 minutes so they sear and develop a deep brown crust. Stir once, then cook another 2 to 3 minutes until golden all over.
  4. Push mushrooms to the edges. Add the garlic and red pepper flakes to the center and cook about 30 seconds until fragrant, then toss everything together.
  5. Pour in the dry sherry or white wine and scrape up any browned bits. Cook until nearly evaporated, about 1 minute.
  6. Add the spinach in two or three handfuls, tossing between additions and letting each batch wilt before adding the next. This should take about 2 minutes total.
  7. Season with kosher salt and black pepper. Squeeze the lemon juice over the top, toss, and finish with fresh parmesan if using. Serve immediately.

Notes

  • 150g of spinach looks like a lot raw, but it wilts down to almost nothing.
  • Add spinach only after the mushrooms are fully browned or it will release water and undo your sear.
  • For a fuller meal, toss with pasta, spoon over polenta, or pile onto crusty bread with a fried egg on top.
Cream variation: stir in 3 tbsp heavy cream after deglazing for a richer, saucier result.
Why it works: Browning the mushrooms first builds deep flavor, then the lemon at the end brightens everything so the spinach stays lively instead of muddy.
