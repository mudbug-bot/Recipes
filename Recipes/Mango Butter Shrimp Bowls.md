# Mango Butter Shrimp Bowls

It's the weekend, so I am going to give you a recipe that has multiple elements; however, you can break this up and save time making just a single element as well. This is one of those dishes that's perfect for feeding a group because it doesn't actually feel like you're feeding a group. Everything goes out in separate components, and everyone builds their own bowl exactly how they want it: more shrimp, extra beans, heavy on the mango butter, light on the rice.

It also scales effortlessly. Add a stack of warm, toasted tortillas on the side, and suddenly it turns into bowls or tacos, depending on the mood (and how many times people go back for seconds).

The best part? Every element stands on its own. The smoky, creamy black beans are a meal by themselves. The mango butter shrimp works just as well over rice, tucked into tortillas, or spooned straight from the pan. The charred tomatoes can live on anything. Put it all together, and it feels special — break it apart, and it still works.

That's my favorite kind of cooking: flexible, forgiving, and built for real life. This is how we eat as a family.

## Components

- Mango butter–glazed shrimp
- Creamy black beans with smoky bacon and red pepper
- Charred cherry tomatoes
- Steamed rice
- Avocado, lime, cotija, ancho chili
- Optional: toasted tortillas for scooping, wrapping, or straight-up snacking

## Creamy Black Beans with Smoky Bacon and Red Pepper

### Ingredients
- 1 lb bacon, sliced
- ½ onion, small dice
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 red bell pepper, small dice
- 2 (12-oz) cans black beans, drained and rinsed
- Splash of beef stock (or water)
- Butter, to finish
- Ancho chili powder
- Kosher salt
- Fresh cracked black pepper

### Method
- Render the sliced bacon in a wide pan until crispy.
- Pour off half the bacon fat (leave the rest — that's flavor but save the residual for cooking eggs or just putting into your hair).
- Add the onion and sauté until soft.
- Add garlic and cook just until fragrant.
- Add diced red pepper and sauté until tender.
- Stir in the black beans and a splash of stock.
- Simmer 5–8 minutes.
- Mash about half the beans against the side of the pan with a ladle to make them creamy.
- Finish with a knob of butter, ancho chili, salt, and fresh cracked pepper.
- Set aside and keep warm.

## Charred Cherry Tomatoes

### Ingredients
- Long cherry tomatoes (or basic cherry tomatoes will work as well)
- Olive oil
- Sherry vinegar or balsamic
- Kosher salt
- Fresh cracked black pepper

### Method
- Heat a carbon steel or cast iron pan until very hot.
- Add a touch of oil and toss in the tomatoes.
- Let them blister and lightly char — don't move them too much.
- Pull off heat and hit with a dash of vinegar, salt, and pepper.
- Set aside.
- Bright, acidic, and just enough char to wake everything up.

## Mango Butter Shrimp

### Ingredients
- 2 lbs shrimp (Argentinian red shrimp work beautifully here)
- Neutral oil
- 4–5 cloves garlic, sliced or minced
- 1 ripe mango, small dice
- Butter
- Lemon juice
- Kosher salt

### Method
- Heat a cast iron or carbon steel pan until blasting hot.
- Add oil, then the shrimp in a single layer.
- Sear hard on both sides until just cooked.
- Add the garlic and toast lightly in the shrimp fat.
- Splash in lemon juice.
- Add the diced mango and toss to coat.
- Finish with butter, mounting the sauce until glossy and rich.
- Season lightly with salt.
- The mango breaks down just enough to create a sweet, buttery glaze — borderline lobster vibes.

## Assembly

- Steamed rice on the bottom
- Creamy black beans on one side
- Mango butter shrimp over the top
- Charred tomatoes scattered throughout or on the side. I also toss the tomatoes in some of the shrimp butter that's left in the pan (pictured above)
- Fresh avocado pieces
- Lime wedges
- Cotija cheese
- Fresh cracked pepper and a pinch of ancho or Aleppo chili to finish

Serve everything family-style and let people go to town. Bowls, tacos, second helpings — all encouraged.

This is the kind of meal that works on a random weeknight or weekend, feeds a crowd without stress, and somehow still feels intentional. Big flavor, simple techniques, and zero pressure to get it "perfect."
