# Homemade Tomato Sauce

Source: Andrew Gruel, Substack – "Stop Buying Jarred Tomato Sauce"

Tomato sauce started as a way to preserve the harvest and stretch food cheaply—not as a supermarket product. Families crushed tomatoes, cooked them briefly, seasoned simply, and used them all week. No added sugar, no long simmer, no marketing.

Making your own tomato sauce is one of the easiest, cheapest, and most useful things you can do in the kitchen. It takes about 15 minutes, uses pantry staples, and tastes better (and costs less) than almost any jar.

## Ingredients

- 1 large can San Marzano–style whole tomatoes (or any whole peeled tomatoes)
- 3 cloves garlic, lightly crushed or pressed
- 3 tablespoons olive oil
- Pinch red pepper flakes or Aleppo pepper (optional)
- 2 tablespoons butter, to finish (optional)
- Sea salt, to taste
- 1–2 tablespoons fresh basil, sliced

## Method

1. **Crush the tomatoes by hand**  
   Pour the tomatoes and their juices into a bowl. Crush gently with your hands into large, irregular pieces.
   - Hand-crushing gives better texture: chunks, pulp, and juice instead of uniform mush.  
   - Avoid breaking too many seeds, which can make the sauce bitter.

2. **Warm the oil and aromatics**  
   Heat the olive oil in a wide pan over medium heat. Add the crushed garlic and a pinch of red pepper flakes (if using). Cook gently until fragrant, about 30–45 seconds.
   - You want the garlic to bloom in the oil, not brown. Browning makes it bitter.

3. **Add tomatoes and bring to a simmer**  
   Add the crushed tomatoes and their juices to the pan. Stir to combine and bring to a steady simmer.
   - A wide pan increases evaporation just enough to concentrate flavor without overcooking.

4. **Simmer briefly**  
   Simmer uncovered for 10–15 minutes, stirring occasionally with a wooden spoon.
   - Modern canned tomatoes are already concentrated. Overcooking dulls acidity and flattens flavor.

5. **Season and finish**  
   Season with sea salt until the sauce tastes like bright, fresh tomatoes—not salt. Off the heat, swirl in the cold butter (if using) for richness, then fold in the fresh basil.
   - Basil is a finishing herb; cooking it kills its aroma.

The finished sauce should be:

- Spoonable, not thick
- Bright, not sweet
- Rustic, not completely smooth

If it looks like jarred sauce, you cooked it too long.

## Why Make Your Own

- **Cheaper:** A can of tomatoes costs less than a jar of sauce and makes more.
- **Better flavor:** Jarred sauce is engineered for shelf life; fresh sauce tastes alive.
- **Control:** No added sugar, stabilizers, or industrial oils—just what you choose to add.
- **Technique:** This teaches you about heat, fat, acidity, and timing—skills that carry into everything else you cook.

## Notes

- Use whole canned tomatoes, preferably San Marzano–style. Whole tomatoes are less processed than crushed or puréed and give better texture and flavor.
- A wooden spoon is ideal: it doesn’t react with acid, doesn’t conduct heat, and is gentle on the tomatoes.
- Long, all-day simmers made sense for watery, inconsistent fresh tomatoes. With good canned tomatoes, shorter cooking preserves brightness.
- The sauce keeps 4–5 days in the fridge and freezes well. It often tastes even better the next day.

## Variations & Add-Ins

Keep the base simple and customize later. Ideas:

- Anchovy: melt one into the oil with the garlic for depth (it won’t taste fishy).
- Chili flake: add with the garlic for a little heat.
- Butter finish: swirl in an extra tablespoon of butter at the end for richness.
- Onion: sweat half a finely diced onion before adding the garlic.
- Parmesan rind: simmer briefly in the sauce, then remove for extra umami.
- Ground meat: brown first, remove, then add back at the end.

Once you make this a couple of times, reaching for a jar will feel unnecessary.
