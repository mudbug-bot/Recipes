# The Lobster Mac & Cheese Everyone Always Asks Me to Make

There are a handful of dishes that follow me everywhere, ones people ask about at the restaurant, at holidays, or when they find out I'm cooking. This lobster mac and cheese is one of them.

It's decadent, and it's over-the-top in the best way. It's unapologetically rich; it's also one of the best holiday side dishes you can put on the table, whether it's next to a prime rib, a ham, or standing on its own.

The key here is restraint where it matters and excess where it counts. This is not a gloppy béchamel-based mac and cheese. It's a cream-forward cheese sauce that stays silky, coats the pasta, and absorbs beautifully as it bakes, without breaking.

And yes: if you don't have lobster, you can absolutely substitute crab or cooked shrimp or even chicken. The rule is simple:
All seafood goes in cooked.
Lobster: cooked.
Crab: cooked.
Shrimp: cooked.

Let's cook.

Cooking for fewer people? Cut everything in half.
Feeding a crowd? Double the amount and use a larger pan.

## Ingredients

### Pasta
- 2 pounds macaroni, cavatappi, or shells
- Water seasoned aggressively with salt (about 1 tablespoon kosher salt per gallon)

### Seafood
- 2 to 2½ pounds cooked lobster meat, chopped
or
- 2 pounds cooked crab meat
or
- 2 pounds cooked shrimp, chopped

### Aromatic Base
- 3 cups dry white wine
- 6 garlic cloves, smashed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme
- Fresh cracked black pepper

### Cream Sauce
- 2½ to 3 quarts heavy whipping cream
- Cornstarch slurry (about 3–4 tablespoons cornstarch mixed with cold water)

### Cheese Blend
- 12 oz smoked gouda, shredded
- 12 oz whole milk mozzarella, freshly shredded
- 8 oz aged cheddar (white or orange), shredded
- ½ cup grated Parmesan (plus more for topping)

### Topping
- 1 cup panko breadcrumbs
- 1 cup crushed potato chips
- Fresh herbs (parsley or thyme)
- Extra shredded cheese (optional but encouraged)

## Instructions

Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil and salt it generously — it should taste like the sea.

Cook the pasta just below al dente. You want it slightly underdone because it's going back into the oven.

Drain immediately and shock under cold running water to stop the cooking completely. Set aside.

This step matters. Overcooked pasta + baked mac and cheese = mush.

In a large pot, add:
- White wine
- Garlic
- Thyme
- Black pepper

Bring to a simmer and reduce until almost dry. You want the aromatics and acidity without raw wine flavor.

Pour in the heavy cream and return to a gentle simmer. Reduce slightly—don't rush it.

Once hot, drizzle in the cornstarch slurry while stirring until the sauce thickens to just past nappe — thicker than coating the back of a spoon, but still fluid. Allow the cornstarch too cook out for a few minutes at a light simmer, constantly stirring

Remove from heat and strain the sauce, discarding the aromatics.

While the cream is still hot:
- Whisk in smoked gouda
- Add mozzarella
- Finish with cheddar

Whisk until smooth, gooey, and fully melted.

Taste. Adjust seasoning if needed.

This should be rich, glossy, and pourable — not stiff.

In a very large bowl:
- Add cold pasta
- Pour over the cheese sauce

Use all of it. The noodles will absorb more than you think.

Fold in your cooked lobster, crab, or shrimp gently.

You want this to look slightly loose and very saucy — that's intentional.

Transfer to a buttered baking dish.

Top with:
- Panko
- Crushed potato chips
- Parmesan
- Extra cheese (because why not)
- Fresh herbs

Bake at 375°F for 30–40 minutes, uncovered, until bubbling and golden - the time may be less, so be sure to check every 10 minutes in the beginning and 5 minutes as it gets brown.

If you want extra color, broil for the last 2–3 minutes — keep an eye on it.

This dish is about confidence. Don't skimp on sauce. Don't overcook the pasta. And don't apologize for how rich it is — that's the point.

It's indulgent. It's celebratory. And it's meant to be shared.

If you're cooking for a holiday table, this is one of those dishes people remember and ask you to make again next year.
