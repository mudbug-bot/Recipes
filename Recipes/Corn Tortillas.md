# Corn Tortillas

Corn tortillas are one of the most elegant examples of simple food done right - just a few simple ingredients: whole corn, lime, water, and heat. A process refined over thousands of years that made corn more nutritious, more digestible, and more flavorful without additives or fortification.

Industrialization changed that.

As tortillas moved from homes and local tortillerias into national supply chains, priorities shifted, as they did with most processed food. Shelf life and distribution replaced freshness, and speed replaced process; ultimately, uniformity also replaced flavor. Corn that was once nixtamalized (read about this process below), slowly with lime, was increasingly dried, refined, and standardized to move faster through factories and survive long distribution routes.

Along the way, nutrients that were naturally available through proper nixtamalization were stripped out or diminished. Keep in mind that this is also what happened with flour and many other foods - instead of preserving the traditional process, the system responded by adding things back later through enrichment and fortification. The ultra processing stripped our ingredients of their natural nutrient profile. That is how we ended up with tortillas that look traditional but are nutritionally and culturally disconnected from how they were originally made.

Corn tortillas do not need enrichment when the process is respected. Nixtamalization already does the work.

## What is Nixtamalization?

Nixtamalization is the traditional process used to prepare corn for tortillas and masa. It has been used for thousands of years in Mesoamerica and is the reason corn became a staple food rather than just another grain.

Dried corn is cooked and soaked in water with lime, also called calcium hydroxide. After soaking, the corn is rinsed and the outer skins are removed. The softened kernels are then ground into masa.

This process makes corn nutritionally complete. It unlocks niacin, a B vitamin that is otherwise unavailable in untreated corn. Without nixtamalization, diets based heavily on corn can lead to serious deficiencies. Cultures that used this process did not suffer those outcomes.

Nixtamalization also improves mineral absorption. The lime contributes calcium and makes other minerals more bioavailable. It improves digestibility by breaking down tough cell walls and changing the starch structure so the dough binds properly. That is why masa holds together and puffs when cooked.

Finally, it creates flavor. The aroma, taste, and structure of a real corn tortilla come from this reaction between corn and lime. When the process is done correctly, tortillas do not need fortification or correction. The nutrition is already there.

## Sourcing Ingredients

Before you start, the most important step is sourcing properly nixtamalized corn. Look for masa harina or fresh masa labeled 100 percent nixtamalized corn, made with lime and nothing else. Avoid products that rely on enrichment or added vitamins to compensate for shortcuts in processing. If you have access to a local tortilleria, ask if they grind their own nixtamal. Fresh masa from a tortilleria is ideal, but a well-labeled masa harina works beautifully for home cooking.

## Ingredients

- 2 cups masa harina (100 percent nixtamalized corn, not enriched)
- 1 and 1/2 cups warm water
- 1/2 teaspoon salt (optional)

## Instructions

- Mix masa harina and salt in a bowl.
- Slowly add warm water, mixing until a soft dough forms.
- Knead gently for 1 to 2 minutes. The dough should feel like soft Play Doh.
- Cover and rest for 10 to 15 minutes.
- Divide into 10 to 12 balls.
- Press between parchment or plastic until thin.
- Heat a dry skillet (cast iron works) or comal over medium-high heat.
- Cook 45 to 60 seconds per side until lightly charred and flexible.
- Stack in a towel to keep warm.

If they puff slightly, you did it right.

## Troubleshooting

- Cracking edges means the dough is too dry. Add a splash of water.
- Sticky dough means it is too wet. Dust lightly with masa.
- No puff usually means the pan is not hot enough or the tortillas are too thick.

## Storage

- Best the same day
- Refrigerate up to 3 days
- Freeze up to 2 months with parchment between tortillas

Reheat on a hot skillet, never straight from the fridge to the plate.

## Label Reading Tips

Look for masa harina labeled:

- 100 percent nixtamalized corn
- No added vitamins or enrichment
- No oils

Read the label. The good stuff is still out there.
