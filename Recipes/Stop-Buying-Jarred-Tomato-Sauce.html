Homemade-Tomato-Sauce

A favorite from Rob's recipe collection.

Side

Ingredients

  • 1 large can San Marzano–style whole tomatoes (or any whole peeled tomatoes)
  • 3 cloves garlic, lightly crushed or pressed
  • 3 tablespoons olive oil
  • Pinch red pepper flakes or Aleppo pepper (optional)
  • 2 tablespoons butter, to finish (optional)
  • Sea salt, to taste
  • 1–2 tablespoons fresh basil, sliced

Method

  1. Crush the tomatoes by hand. Pour the tomatoes and their juices into a bowl. Crush gently with your hands into large, irregular pieces. Avoid breaking too many seeds, which can make the sauce bitter.
  2. Warm the oil and aromatics. Heat the olive oil in a wide pan over medium heat. Add the crushed garlic and a pinch of red pepper flakes (if using). Cook gently until fragrant, about 30–45 seconds, without browning the garlic.
  3. Add tomatoes and bring to a simmer. Add the crushed tomatoes and their juices to the pan. Stir to combine and bring to a steady simmer. A wide pan helps concentrate flavor without overcooking.
  4. Simmer briefly. Simmer uncovered for 10–15 minutes, stirring occasionally with a wooden spoon. Modern canned tomatoes are already concentrated; you’re just bringing them together, not cooking them down all day.
  5. Season and finish. Season with sea salt until the sauce tastes like bright tomatoes, not salt. Off the heat, swirl in the butter (if using) for richness, then fold in the fresh basil. Do not cook the basil; it’s a finishing herb.

What You’re Looking For

  • Spoonable, not thick like paste
  • Bright and fresh, not sugary
  • Rustic texture, not completely smooth
If it looks and tastes like typical jarred sauce, you probably cooked it too long or added too much sugar. This should feel alive and tomato-forward.

Why Make Your Own

  • Cheaper: A single can of tomatoes makes more sauce than most jars at a lower cost per cup.
  • Better flavor: Jarred sauce is engineered for shelf life; this is cooked for tonight’s dinner.
  • Control: No added sugar, stabilizers, or industrial oils—unless you put them in.
  • Technique: Teaches you heat, fat, acidity, and timing—core skills that carry over into everything else.

Notes & Variations

  • Use whole canned tomatoes, preferably San Marzano–style, for best texture and flavor.
  • Stir with a wooden spoon to avoid metallic off-flavors and to be gentle on the tomatoes.
  • The sauce keeps 4–5 days in the fridge and freezes well; it often tastes better the next day.

Optional Add-Ins

  • Anchovy: melt one into the oil with the garlic for depth (it won’t make it taste fishy).
  • Extra chili flake: add with the garlic for a bit more heat.
  • Onion: sweat half a finely diced onion before adding the garlic.
  • Parmesan rind: simmer in the sauce briefly, then remove for extra umami.
  • Ground meat: brown first, remove, then add back at the end for a quick ragù.
Summary: For less than the cost of a single jar of “premium” sauce, you get more volume, better flavor, and complete control—plus a skill you’ll use for life.
