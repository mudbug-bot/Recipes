# Chicken Sausage, Braised Chicken Thigh & Red Rice

![Main Image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MJlS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F71bf5d65-6544-446a-974a-c6b493e279ef_1620x1080.jpeg)

There's been a lot of noise online lately about whether it's possible to eat on $15 a day. Of course, that number assumes food—not delivery fees, Starbucks lattes, bags of Takis, or the other conveniences that have quietly become staples of American consumerism.

I find the debate fascinating because there's a loud segment of people who genuinely believe it's impossible to eat within that budget outside of something resembling prison food. Anyone who's followed my cooking for years knows this misses the point entirely. Not only is it possible to eat well on a limited budget, but in many cases, the less expensive, underutilized cuts of protein, vegetables, and grains are actually more flavorful. They just ask more of the cook.

That's the tradeoff. When ingredients get cheaper, technique becomes the asset. Flavor doesn't come from the price tag - it comes from browning, patience, heat control, and knowing how to coax depth out of humble ingredients.

It's also why the center-plate dishes that go viral in restaurants are rarely filet or ribeye. They're braised short ribs, chuck shoulder, country pork chops—soups and stews that punch far above their weight. These dishes carry memory, richness, and identity in a way a perfectly seared filet rarely does.

This recipe is built on that exact principle: how do you create a meal for under $5 per person that still tastes like somewhere you want to be?

Braised chicken thighs, rice, and smoked chicken sausage might be simple on paper, but together they're the boldest expression of value cooking I know.

This dish hits every part of the palate without leaning on expensive ingredients. The chicken thighs are browned hard and then gently braised, which means you get both savory depth and tenderness without drying the meat out. The sausage brings smoke, salt, and spice—doing the heavy lifting of seasoning the entire pan. And the rice? It's not just filler. Cooked in stock and tomato paste, it absorbs every ounce of rendered fat and spice, turning into something closer to a pilaf than a side dish.

The tomato paste is the quiet hero here. Cooked until it turns dark and sweet, it creates a deep, brick-red base that tastes far richer than its ingredient list suggests. Paprika and cumin add warmth rather than heat, while white beans melt into the rice, giving the dish a creamy, almost stew-like body without cream or butter.

What you end up with is comfort food that tastes intentional: smoky, savory, gently spiced, and deeply satisfying. It's the kind of dish where each bite tastes better than the last—not because it's heavy, but because the flavors are layered and balanced.

This is how you stretch a dollar without sacrificing pleasure—by letting technique do the work. Brown the meat properly. Cook the tomato paste until it changes. Toast the rice. Let the pan do its thing. None of this is complicated, but all of it matters.

Below is the full recipe. I've included the cost breakdown so you can see exactly how this comes in under five dollars per person without cutting corners. Keep in mind these are California prices, so they will most likely be much lower in other areas.

## One-Pan Weeknight Dinner (3–5 servings)

- 1.25 lb boneless chicken thighs
- 1 lb andouille sausage
- 1 cup long-grain white rice
- 1 can cannellini or white beans, drained and rinsed
- 1 small onion, diced
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 tbsp tomato paste
- 2¼ cups chicken stock (hot)
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 1 tsp paprika (smoked if possible)
- ½ tsp cumin
- Pinch red pepper flakes
- Kosher salt and black pepper
- Optional finish: chopped parsley or a squeeze of lemon

## Instructions

### Beginning steps (finishing below)

#### Brown the chicken
Pat the chicken thighs dry and season generously with salt and pepper. Heat olive oil in a wide, heavy skillet over medium-high heat. Brown the thighs until deeply golden, 4–5 minutes per side. Remove and set aside.

#### Brown the sausage
In the same pan, add the sliced sausage. Let it take on color, then remove. Don't wipe the pan—you want those browned bits.

#### Build the red base
Add the onion and cook until soft and lightly caramelized. Add garlic and tomato paste. Cook the paste until it turns dark brick-red and smells sweet, 2–3 minutes. This step matters.

#### Toast the rice
Stir in paprika, cumin, and red pepper flakes. Add the rice and stir until glossy and coated, about 30–60 seconds.

#### Braise and simmer
Add hot stock, scraping up the bottom of the pan. Nestle the chicken thighs back in, scatter the sausage around them, cover, and bring to a simmer. Reduce heat to low and cook 18–20 minutes, until the rice is tender.

#### Finish with beans
Stir in the drained beans during the last 5 minutes, just to warm through and bind the dish.

#### Rest and serve
Turn off heat and let the pan rest, covered, for 5 minutes. Fluff the rice, finish with black pepper, herbs, or lemon if you like, and serve straight from the skillet.

This is real food, cooked with intention, that respects both flavor and budget. No tricks—just fundamentals.
