# The Meatloaf That Works Twice

There are two kinds of meatloaf. The first is the one most of us grew up with: free-formed on a sheet tray, baked until done, glazed, sliced, eaten, forgotten.

The second is the one I created for the restaurant because it slices perfectly, stays juicy, and turns into something arguably better the next day.

We're making the second one.

The secret is structure and moisture management.

That means a real panade, properly cooked aromatics, and shaping the loaf into a tight cylinder before it ever hits the oven.

Do this right and you get:

- tender texture instead of crumbly
- meat that stays juicy
- slices that hold
- a roast you can repurpose into sandwiches or sliders

And instead of the old sweet-ketchup profile, we lean towards Italian meatball: basil, olive oil, garlic, tomato. So essentially this is a meatball meatloaf.

Breadcrumbs are dry. They pull moisture out of the meat as it cooks.

A panade (bread + milk + egg) is already hydrated. It becomes a paste that traps juices and emulsifies with the fat. Think tender meatball, not cafeteria brick.

It's the difference between:

compacted ground meat

and

something plush and sliceable.

While the bread soaks, you cook your onions and garlic.

Raw onion in meatloaf = sharp, watery, inconsistent.

Cooked onion = sweet, integrated, professional.

Let them cool slightly before mixing so they don't tighten the proteins.

## Ingredients

- 1 lb ground beef
- ½–1 lb ground pork
- 3 slices crustless white bread
- ½ cup whole milk
- 1 egg
- 1 small onion, fine dice
- 5 cloves garlic, minced
- 2–3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 2 tablespoon worcestershire sauce
- ½ cup tomato sauce (plus more for glazing)
- Handful of fresh basil, chopped
- Pinch dried oregano
- Salt
- Fresh black pepper
- honey for the glaze

## Instructions

Mash bread, milk, and egg into a paste.

Let it fully hydrate, 5–10 minutes.

Sweat onion in olive oil with a pinch of salt until soft and slightly sweet.

Add garlic, cook 30 seconds.

Cool.

Into the panade goes:

- cooked onion & garlic
- basil
- oregano
- tomato sauce
- Worcestershire sauce
- salt & pepper

Mix that first. Make it uniform.

Fold in beef and pork gently.

Don't knead like dough.

Mix until just combined.

Let it sit 10–15 minutes so the bread absorbs and the proteins relax.

Lay out two long sheets of plastic wrap.

Form the meat into a log and roll it tight, like a giant sausage.

Twist the ends to compress.

Wrap that whole thing in foil.

Why this matters:

- even thickness
- better cooking
- professional slices later
- no blowouts

Bake until the center hits 155°F.

Rest it. Carryover will finish the job.

Warm extra tomato sauce with:

- butter
- a touch of honey

Unwrap the loaf.

Brush generously.

Back into the oven, now on a rack, until sticky, shiny, browned.

This is where it becomes special.

Slice thick.

More sauce if you want.

Done.

Stand a slice upright.

Sauce.

Melt mozzarella or provolone over the top.

Knife and fork, restaurant energy.

Chill the whole loaf.

Slice cold (it'll be perfect).

Sear in butter until crusty, then cover to steam through.

Hero sandwich. Slider party. Lunch solved.

You cook once but eat twice.

It's economical, practical, and it respects the original purpose of dishes like meatloaf: stretching meat without sacrificing satisfaction.

Leftovers weren't an accident.
