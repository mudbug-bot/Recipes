# Eggs in the Hole

![Main Image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AXFr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7e7f007d-31ff-46ac-a234-ae995dcb221b_1620x1080.jpeg)

A friend posted a picture of eggs in toast on X the other day, and I sarcastically pointed out that this dish would be $29.99 at any brunch restaurant in LA. Out of curiosity, I looked it up—and sure enough, a few chic spots really are charging over twenty bucks for it. And honestly, I get why this simple dish comes off as fancy in restaurants.

Even though it's only three main ingredients - eggs, bread, and butter - it reads fancy. It's minimal and confident. This is one of those dishes you can make in a pinch as a snack or serve to friends that quietly says, I know how to cook, without sending you to the store for a hundred specialty ingredients and some micro arugula.

I love dishes like this; the ones that rely entirely on a few basics, good heat control, and restraint. You don't need a fancy sauce, or a bed of greens, just an understanding of heat and a good pan.

## What You Need

- Thick-cut bread (sourdough or country loaf)
- 2–4 eggs
- Unsalted Butter (real butter)
- Salt and freshly cracked black pepper
- A good chunk of Parmesan, micro-planed at the end

### Optional:
- Freshly sliced chives or scallions
- Hot sauce or jam on the side

## Instructions

### 1. Cut the hole, keep the plug
Use a glass to punch a clean hole in the bread. Save the cut-out—it cooks alongside and never goes to waste.

### 2. Butter the pan—and the detail that matters
Set a skillet over medium-low heat and melt butter. Don't let the butter brown, just allow it to foam a bit. Here is where people screw up, they let the pan get too hot. Lay the bread and the cut-out into the pan so they slowly absorb it.

Before the egg goes in, brush melted butter along the inside edge of the cut-out. That inner rim is where most versions fail. Buttering it lets the bread fry instead of steam once the egg goes in. Flip the bread so the toasted side is showing, then add the egg.

### 3. Add the egg and season early
Crack the egg directly into the center. Salt and pepper immediately. Lower the heat.

### 4. Steam, don't flip
Add a small dash of water to the pan—just enough to create steam, not puddles. Cover the pan slightly.

The steam sets the whites gently while the yolk stays soft and rich. No flipping, no broken yolks, no drama. BUT you don't want it to steam too long, otherwise the bread will get soggy, so you may even need to add the cover, then take it off and add again for a few seconds until the yolk gets to your liking.

### 5. Finish with fresh butter
Once the whites are set, uncover the pan and add a small knob of fresh butter. Let the bread finish toasting and crisping in the fat.

This step matters. It restores texture and deepens flavor.

### 6. Shower with Parmesan
Kill the heat. While everything is still hot, micro-plane Parmesan generously over the top.

The cheese melts instantly, clings to the egg and bread, and seasons every bite without weighing it down. It's not garnish—it's part of the dish.

## Key Points

- Steam cooks the egg without overcooking the yolk
- Buttered edges stay crisp, not soggy
- Finishing in fresh butter brings everything back together
- Micro-planed Parmesan melts instead of clumps

Nothing is rushed. Nothing is forced.

## Variations

- Chives or scallions for freshness
- Hot sauce for contrast
- Jam on the side if you like sweet against savory

But honestly, eggs, bread, butter, and Parmesan is a complete sentence.

This is the kind of breakfast that proves good cooking isn't about complexity—it's about knowing when to stop.
